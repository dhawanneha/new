ZipTrip

Designing a real time carpooling app for students at University of Michigan

Methods

User Interviews, Persona, Crazy Eight, Storyboarding, Journey Mapping, Sketching, Paper Prototyping, & Low Fidelity Prototype

Duration

12 weeks

Role

UX Designer


Project

Individual


Problem Statement

This individual project stemmed from my own experiences of inefficient and inconvenient commute to college campus or grocery shopping especially on weekends.

I have observed students often wait up to half an hour for the bus after purchasing groceries. Most of them generally have a commute of 15 minutes or less but they usually cannot walk back comfortably with their groceries. The Ride buses are the most used method of commute for students (besides the blue buses). The bus rides are free but they are infrequent on weekends and during the late hours which makes commute inconvenient. While there are University of Michigan blue buses that run inside the campus, the students who live off campus do not have another affordable option besides Ride buses. Therefore, the lack of frequent public transportation in Ann Arbor results in a massive wastage of student's time.

Validation of the Problem

I validated the problem statement among the student community by conducting seven interviews with students. I asked them about commute related frustrations. The major themes that recurred in these interviews were grocery shopping. So, I asked them about their grocery habits, process for grocery shopping, time taken to do groceries, and means of transportation. The problem validation phase made me realize that commute on the whole could be improved. Grocery shopping was the major pain point of the students.

Target Audience

Target Audience Based on findings from my interviews, I streamlined primary audience of my project as students without a car who require better solution to do groceries or commute in affordable manner.

Competitive Analysis

After streamlining my problem statement and audience, my next step was to identify existing solutions and analyze them to evaluate Competitive analysis helped me evaluate how students are currently solving their problems. It helped me find gaps in the existing solutions and made me realize opportunity areas. I considered TheRide( bus service), Uber, Zipcar, InstaCart (grocery delivery app), ArborBike (Bike Sharing program), and IShareaRide (Carpooling for work).

The solutions included a robot to do groceries, 3D printer for groceries, grocery trucks, carpooling, suction pipe delivery of groceries, campus grocery store, self-driven blue bus for groceries, heated pathways with carts and bikes on hire.

Research

According to Census Bureau, 2015:

  • More than 40% households in Ann Arbor have 2 cars and more than 33% of the household have one car.
  • The most common method to commute in Ann Arbor as adopted by 55% of the population is driving alone.

Ann Arbor is a college town where two types of car owners might be interested in carpooling:

  • The students who would like to have company and also make some extra money by carpooling.
  • The working population who might want to carpool to meet new people, help students, and reduce carbon print.

Design Rationale

I used Questions, Options, and Criteria to come up with the features of the app. It made me think critically about each way to solve a problem via different features to evaluate and compare all my options.

Final Design

ZipTrip is an on-demand carpooling app where car owners offer a ride and riders request for a ride in the real-time. Due to time constraints, I only created a prototype for the riders because they are my primary audience. On-demand carpooling in real-time as a solution would be beneficial for not just the grocers but also students who need to get around in Ann Arbor.

Description Of How Car Owner And Riders Are Matched

The following section explains how matching of riders and car owners is done:

  • Location based matching: The current location and destination is considered to match both parties. The rider and car owner are matched if they are located within 0.5 miles of each other, and they are going in the same direction. The car owners would not drive more than 1 mile outside their route to drop the rider.
  • Number of seats: The car owners list the number of seats on offer and the riders specify the number of seats required. If the car owner has 3 available seats and rider requires 2, then it is a match. However, if riders require 3 and car owner has 2 seats to offer, then it is not a match.
  • Bid for a ride: The riders and car owners bid for a particular ride. Both parties can view the average price of the trip listed by the other party which gives a decent idea to users to bid for a ride.
  • Pick up point and drop off point: The app generates pick up points and drop off points that are convenient for both parties. These points are located within 0.25 miles of the rider which takes 5 minutes of walk. However, it is possible that these points may sometime coincide with actual location and destination of the rider.

Societal Impact

ZipTrip might help in saving time and money for students. It would provide an affordable option to the students besides the Ride to commute. If car owners are students, they might make some pocket money by carpooling and meet other new students. Other residents and working professionals might have sense of fulfilment by giving rides to people who need it and help in saving energy and reducing carbon footprint. Also, since Ann Arbor has huge international student population, this app will promote conversations among people from different cultural and national backgrounds.
